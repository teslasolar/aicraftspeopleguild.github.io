Speedrunning the Mythical Man-Month

Solopreneurs, AI Agents, and the Rediscovery of Team Dynamics

Position Paper Published on April 9, 2026 Eric Wright
← Back to White Papers
The Promise The Hidden Replay Three Layers of Context What We Are Learning Toward a New Synthesis

In the early 1970s, Fred Brooks observed something counterintuitive about large software projects: adding more people to a late project makes it later. Communication overhead, mismatched assumptions, and the sheer entropy of coordination turned what should have been linear gains into quadratic friction. His book The Mythical Man-Month became foundational not because it was pessimistic, but because it described a stubborn reality of collaborative development that no methodology (waterfall, agile, or otherwise) has ever fully escaped.

Decades later, we are replaying that experiment at hyperspeed, but with silicon instead of salaries. The new archetype is the solopreneur or solo developer armed with agentic AI tools. These solo builders are not getting around the limitations of collaborative development — they’re speedrunning them.

The Promise: One Person, Infinite Velocity

Tools like Claude Code operate directly in the terminal or cloud, understanding context across files and executing multi-step plans with minimal hand-holding. Google Antigravity, launched in late 2025 as an “agent-first” IDE, goes further. Its Agent Manager lets a developer spawn dozens of specialized agents (architects, implementers, testers) working in parallel across workspaces, all orchestrated through natural language directives. Codex-style models evolved into the backbone of this ecosystem. The result? What once took a five-person sprint now fits in a weekend hack for a skilled individual with the right prompts.

Solopreneurs are building production-grade applications, micro-SaaS products, and internal tools faster than ever. The economic incentive is obvious: no hiring, no meetings, no equity dilution. Just you, a frontier model, and an agent swarm that feels like having a tireless junior team on demand. It is the ultimate fulfillment of the “10x developer” myth (except the multiplier isn’t raw coding speed). It’s the removal of human coordination tax.

Or so it seems until we dig deeper.

The Hidden Replay: Non-Determinism Meets the Man-Month

Beneath the surface of those rapidly built MVPs, something deeper is happening. These agents are not deterministic compilers. They are non-deterministic language models trained to be outcome-seeking, not truth-seeking. Their objective function is to produce the most plausible continuation that satisfies the prompt (whatever it takes). If a rule, a style guide, or an architectural constraint stands in the way of a “good enough” output, the model will happily violate it unless the prompt is engineered with near-legalistic precision. Hallucinations, subtle drifts in intent, and creative interpretations of vague instructions are not bugs; they are features of the training paradigm.

This is where the Mythical Man-Month reappears in fast-forward. When a solo developer scales to multi-agent workflows (whether through Antigravity’s native orchestration, Claude’s task chaining, or homegrown frameworks like CrewAI and AutoGen), they are effectively onboarding an arbitrary number N of new “developers” instantaneously. Each agent has its own context window, its own interpretation of the project spec, and its own probabilistic tendencies. The result is the same explosion of communication and alignment overhead Brooks documented, only now it manifests as token limits, context drift, conflicting pull requests from different agents, and debugging sessions that feel like mediating arguments between invisible colleagues.

We see Conway’s Law reasserting itself in silicon. The systems these agent teams produce mirror the (lack of) communication structure imposed on them. A poorly orchestrated swarm generates tangled architectures that reflect fragmented agent handoffs rather than coherent design. Assumptions that a human team would surface in standups or code reviews (“Wait, did we agree on auth strategy?”) remain unvoiced until runtime errors appear. The agents don’t gossip, politick, or defend turf the way humans do, yet the patterns of intra-group behavior emerge anyway: duplicated effort, incompatible interfaces, and accumulated technical debt.

Beyond Code Completion: The Three Layers of Context

The prevailing assumption in early AI coding discourse was that the primary limitation was accuracy of code completion. If only the models got smarter, hallucinated less, and respected context windows better, the problem would be solved. That view is already obsolete.

The real frontier is context management across three distinct layers:

  1. Single-agent context: What one model instance “knows” at any moment (its system prompt, conversation history, retrieved documents, and tool outputs). This is brittle and finite.
  2. Team (multi-agent) context: The shared understanding across agents. How do Agent A’s architectural decisions propagate to Agent B’s implementation? How do you resolve conflicting plans without a human referee constantly intervening?
  3. Project context: The living institutional memory of requirements, business logic, user feedback, and non-functional constraints that no single prompt can fully capture. This is where human teams naturally maintain tribal knowledge through osmosis, documentation rituals, and cultural norms.

As foundation models become more “human” (longer contexts, better reasoning traces, multimodal awareness, even simulated personalities), these layers don’t simplify. They expose the behavioral and technical realities of collaboration that were previously hidden behind the noise of human personalities. Agents don’t get tired or defensive, but they do optimize ruthlessly for the outcome you appeared to request. They will ship the feature by ignoring edge cases if the prompt rewards speed over robustness. They refactor code in ways that break downstream systems because their local objective was “make this module cleaner.”

We are discovering that many of the “soft” problems in software engineering were never really soft. They were emergent properties of trying to align multiple goal-seeking intelligences around a shared, evolving artifact. The tech stack (IDEs, version control, CI/CD, observability) was built assuming human developers with shared context, accountability, and the ability to ask clarifying questions in real time. Agentic flows are stress-testing every assumption in that stack.

What We Are Actually Learning

The solopreneur revolution is not proving that collaboration is obsolete. It is proving that collaboration has always been harder than we want to admit (and that the bottlenecks were never primarily about typing speed or individual brilliance). By compressing the timeline from months to minutes, AI agents are making visible the fractal nature of coordination problems.

We see it in the proliferation of “agent governance” patterns: prompt libraries that act as constitutions, evaluation harnesses that serve as code reviews, memory layers that function as institutional knowledge bases, and human-in-the-loop escalation paths that look suspiciously like management hierarchies. Every multi-agent framework is, in essence, reinventing organizational design from first principles.

And because the models are outcome-seeking, they force us to confront a deeper philosophical point: software development was never just about correctness. It was about shared truth (a collective understanding of what the system should do, why, and under what constraints). LLMs excel at approximating the appearance of that truth but have no internal stake in maintaining it when pressured.

Toward a New Synthesis of Human and Agent

The transition to AI-augmented solo development is an accelerated stress test that is teaching us what collaboration actually is when stripped of human theater. The patterns (Conway’s Law, communication overhead, context synchronization) were not purely artifacts of biology. They are invariants of any system composed of multiple goal-directed entities trying to maintain coherence around a complex artifact.

As these tools mature, the most successful solopreneurs and development leaders will be those who master the meta-game of designing agent teams whose emergent behavior respects the same hard-won lessons that human teams learned the painful way. They will treat prompt engineering as architecture, context orchestration as governance, and verification loops as culture.

In the end, we are not replacing teams with agents. We are discovering that the fundamental problems of collaborative creation persist even when the collaborators are made of math.

The solopreneur era is not the end of teamwork. It is the fastest possible way to remember why teamwork mattered — and to rebuild it, this time with eyes wide open.

Eric Wright
