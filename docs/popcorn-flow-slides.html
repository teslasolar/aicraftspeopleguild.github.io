Why it matters

The board is not just for tracking progress

The core argument is simple: work starts with real challenges, and the best response is experimentation that produces feedback.

1

Make problems and observations visible instead of treating them as background noise.

2

Generate multiple options before locking into one direction.

3

Use experiments to gather evidence, not to prove that the first idea was right.

Key takeaway

The board helps us see the work. The learning comes from experiments, review, and deciding what to explore next.

Reading the capital letters left to right spells POPCORN, making the sequence easy to recall in conversation.

Front end of the flow

Start with a challenge, then widen before you narrow

P

Problems & Observations

Capture what changed, what hurts, or what is not well understood.

O

Options

Split the problem into multiple possible responses instead of assuming a single answer.

P

Possible Experiments

Turn the chosen option into testable experiments.

Each possible experiment should define

Action What will we do?
Duration How long will it run?
Expectation What outcome do we expect to see?

Learning loop

Takeaways

Five ideas to leave with the team

1

Surface real problems and observations first.

2

Keep multiple options visible before choosing one.

3

Design experiments with action, duration, and expectation.

4

Limit ongoing work so feedback arrives faster.

5

Use review and next-step learning to keep the flow alive.
